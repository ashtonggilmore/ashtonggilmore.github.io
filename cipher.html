Featured Project

Caesar Cipher Wheel Activity

Graphic Design CAD Affinity Designer 3D Print Quick Guide Education

A hands-on cryptography activity for young students — a 3D-printed Caesar cipher wheel paired with a custom quick-reference guide, designed to make encoding and decoding approachable and fun.

Overview

The Caesar Cipher Wheel is an educational activity designed to introduce young students to the concept of cryptography through a tactile, physical tool. The two-disc wheel lets students rotate the inner ring to set a shift value and then encode or decode messages letter by letter.

The project covers the full production pipeline: 3D modelling the wheel in Fusion 360, printing and testing multiple tolerance iterations, and designing a companion quick-reference guide in Affinity Designer that walks students through their first cipher without an adult's help.

Process

How it came together

01

Concept & Sizing

The wheel needed to be large enough for young hands to grip and rotate comfortably without being bulky on a desk. A 100 mm outer diameter was settled on after paper prototyping the letter spacing around the circumference.

02

CAD in Fusion 360

The outer ring and inner disc were modelled as separate bodies. Parameters drove the letter pitch so the 26-character alphabet could be redistributed cleanly if the diameter changed. A snap-fit axle kept assembly tool-free.

03

Print Iteration

Three iterations dialled in the slip-fit clearance between the disc and ring. Too tight and students couldn't rotate it; too loose and letters drifted mid-decode. Final clearance: 0.3 mm per side.

04

Quick-Reference Guide

The companion guide was laid out in Affinity Designer as a folded A5 card. One side explains what a Caesar cipher is; the other walks through encoding and decoding a sample message step by step, with space for the student to write their own.

05

Visual Design

The guide uses a clean, high-contrast layout with large type and numbered steps. Colour coding ties the wheel's two rings to the corresponding "plain" and "cipher" columns in the written examples, reducing cognitive load.

06

Student Testing

A small group of students ran through the activity with the guide and no adult prompting. Their feedback pointed to one ambiguous step in the decoding instructions — corrected in the final print run.

Why 3D print instead of laser cut? The rotating joint between the two discs is trivial to produce with a snap-fit print but would require a separate fastener on a flat laser-cut version — adding assembly steps and cost for a classroom-quantity item.
